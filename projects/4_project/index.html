Temporal Networks

Interaction-driven modeling of evolving networks and epidemic spreading.

From human (social and organizational) to physical and biological systems, complex systems of interacting elements can be modeled as interaction networks — nodes represent elements and edges represent their interactions. As interactions change with time, so do the networks, opening rich avenues for research.

Our work develops interaction-driven temporal models and studies their dynamics: the trendiness of nodes in evolving networks, size-agnostic change-point detection, engagement inequality over time, and interaction-driven contagion. During the COVID-19 pandemic we used these models to assess individual transmission risk, evaluate social-distancing policies, and analyze competition among viral strains.

Representative work includes the interaction-driven temporal transmission model (Marmor et al., 2023), evaluation of social-distancing policies (Mokryn et al., 2024), competition among viral strains (Abbey et al., 2022), the role of trends in growing networks (Mokryn et al., 2016), and size-agnostic change-point detection (Miller & Mokryn, 2020).
