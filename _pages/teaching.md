## Teaching Philosophy

I design courses as active, discussion-driven spaces. My graduate courses use a flipped-classroom model: students prepare through readings and reflections, and class time is devoted to discussion, collaboration, and critical application. I ask students to anticipate unintended consequences, identify design risks, and exercise ethical leadership without formal authority — skills that carry over directly into research and practice.

## Current Courses

**Ethical AI Design of Sociotechnical Systems** *(214.3180)*
: The ethical design of sociotechnical AI systems, focusing on integrating generative AI tools into Human–Computer Interaction design. Students examine sociotechnical systems, stakeholder-centered design, and value-sensitive approaches, and learn to leverage generative AI for ethical, user-centered innovation. A flipped classroom built around theory, case studies, and a culminating group project.

**Data Networks** *(214.2900)*
: An introduction to how the Internet works — networks, protocols, client–server models, and routing. The course centers on the TCP/IP protocol suite and provides hands-on experience with client–server UDP and TCP programming.

**Seminar on Manipulation and Fake News**
: The mechanisms behind digital manipulation and fake news — psychological, technological, and social strategies used to influence public perception, including the role of AI, filter bubbles, disinformation campaigns, and cognitive biases. Students develop analytical tools through critical reading, presentations, and discussion.

## Previous Courses

**Social Network Analysis (SNA)** *(214.3550)*
: The structure of social and complex networks and the mechanisms that govern connected systems — from the growth of the Web to the spread of information, epidemics, and financial crises. Based in part on Easley &amp; Kleinberg, *Networks, Crowds, and Markets* (Cambridge University Press, 2010).
